Highlights of Results

The Met
  • Finding #1: The participant went through the menu bar at the top of the page but noted that none of the categories sounded like they were related to internships. She used the quick-link at the bottom of the page to complete the task.
  • Recommendation: Refine the menu categories to make it clearer and more intuitive for users to find information related to internships at The Met.
  • Task: You're an undergraduate student who wants to explore more about the internships offered through The Met. LMWWYWD.
Bliss
  • Finding #1: The user looked through the menu bar to find her personal routine. She first clicked 'skincare' and then 'bodycare'. Eventually, she clicked 'Find Your Routine' and proceeded to fill out her preferences.
  • Recommendation: Change the label from 'Find Your Routine' to 'Personalize Your Routine' for clarity.
  • Task: Imagine you want to get results of finding products that best fit your skin type. LMWWYWD.
  • Finding #2: The participant got overwhelmed from all of the options on the menu tab when trying to find a lip product. She went to the search, and there was only one product that came up, even though there are other products listed under the "eye & lip" category.
  • Recommendation: Separate the "eye & lip" category to make it easier for users to find lip products.
  • Task: You're in the market for any new lip product. Find a lip product you would want, add it to your cart, and proceed to checkout. LMWWYWD.

What I Learned

This journey has taught me the importance of user feedback in design, the effectiveness of iterative testing, and the value of data-driven decision-making in UX.

September:

  • Heuristic evaluations of The Met website using various frameworks and tools.
  • Discussion of heuristic analysis findings, identifying strengths and weaknesses of The Met's website.
  • Competitive analysis of other museum websites, focusing on usability features and functionalities.
  • Discussion of competitive analysis findings, comparing The Met to its competitors.

October:

  • Writing usability test scripts with clear tasks and scenarios for participants.
  • Pre-testing and revising the usability test scripts to ensure smooth execution.
  • Running usability sessions with participants, observing their interactions and recording their feedback.
  • Analyzing usability data from the sessions, identifying major findings and patterns.

Late October-Early November:

  • Preparing for presentation: compiling video recordings, capturing transcripts, and summarizing findings.
  • Writing up findings and recommendations for presentation, focusing on actionable insights for The Met.
  • Finalizing presentations and practicing delivery.
  • Presenting usability testing findings to the class.
  • Continued presentations.

Late November-December:

  • Conducting in-depth interviews with users to gain a deeper understanding of their needs and frustrations.
  • Analyzing interview data, identifying themes and recurring patterns.
  • Creating affinity diagrams to visually organize interview findings.
  • Developing user personas based on research insights, representing different user types.
  • Discussing personas and refining them based on class feedback.
  • Journey mapping, visualizing user experiences with The Met, from initial contact to post-visit.
  • Conducting card sorts and tree tests to understand how users categorize and navigate information.
  • Discussing card sort data and analyzing how users organize content on The Met website.
  • Exploring human behavior research in UX design, focusing on methods like eye tracking and biometrics.
  • Content strategy: planning content that meets user needs and achieves The Met's goals.
  • Discussing content strategy approaches and identifying potential improvements for The Met's website.
  • Design personas: creating personas specifically for The Met's website audience.
